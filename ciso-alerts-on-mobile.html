CISO alerts for unusual activities

CISO's are able to get alerts via push notification for unusual activities.

The alert was the first of its kind, it derives from two unusual behaviors on the relationships: user-location and user-file.

Framing

Identify user / business needs

People in the role of CISO (Chief Information Security Officer) care for one thing above else, to protect the organization assets from getting on the wrong hands or destroyed, therefore it's very important to alert them about potential risks, and present accurate and valuable information.

Product definition

The main goal is to reach the CISO with urgency to present the relevant information and to enable the option to share this alert, knowing that the mobile device is the one that people check more often and even has ways to push a notification so, we could asume the CISO would look into it shortly.

Process

Brainstorming

The implementation of the alerts on the mobile app is an extension of a feature I collaborated with before, the information displayed was the result of ongoing discussions with stake-holders about the meaning of risk in an enviroment such as the Kiteworks platform.

The mobile design had to adapt a new view that will only be available for some types of users, fortunatelly the mobile main navigation had been recently simplified, so this wasn't a problem.

User research

Based on the hypothesis the team met with internal and external stake-holders, such as the CEO and other excecutives as well as some customers that showed interest on these type of tools before, the goal early on was to get insights into their motivations and expectations and later on to validate versions of the prototype.

Problem / Solution validation

The primary question we had was the types of risks that they were looking for to see if there is a match or if there is something else. Then, the questions were more about the alert we had in mind to qualify its merit according to this user.

Team alignment

I served as the link between the team working on the AI to detect alerts and the teams working on the mobile apps. Meeting regularly with both teams helped everyone to be on the same page.

The spec documentation and the prototype helps bring everything and everyone together, giving everyone access to the design, the ability to ask questions and make comments.

Design

Explorations

In an effort to streamline the information to the user and provide her with the ability to act on that information, we thought about adding a button to share, however after testing with internal stake-holders we discovered that the user needs to see the full details about the alert before making the decision to share.

Idea of expand push notification for options, to see details or share alert.

When capturing the details that the alert needed to cointain including the metadata, it was clear that some repetition could be avoided. We also got feedback about a better display of the location, to get a better understanding of where the alert was triggered.

Idea of full alert details including the files that triggered the alert.

In an effort to create the tools for the user to find alerts quickly, this design shows a dedicated text search field to narrow down search results. Turns out that after some testing with internal stake-holders we realized that the amount of alerts was going to be small and the frequency sparse so a single search filter was enough.

Idea of search and filter alerts.

Iterations

The idea of filtering as an end goal was correct, so we explored ways to simplify the ability to see the query and the filters without the cludder of the three text boxes. We did not explore further given that more alerts would take time to develop and nothing much complicated was going to be needed at that moment.

Iteration on the search alerts interaction.

When thinking about the options for each alert in the context of risk management, we explored the type of actions that the CISO would want to take on the user who triggered the alert, however given that the level of confidence can't be determined on the alerts, we decided not to expand on these ideas for the moment.

Iteration on the alert actions' menu.

This iteration was designed to test the option of not investing on any search, filter or sort capabilities, however given that the engineering effort was small and the amount of alerts was expected to be low, this iteration was discarted.

Iteration of a version without any search, filter or sort capabilities.

In an effort to explore ideas around the main metaphore of alerts, one idea early on was to enable a snooze option assuming the system would somehow insist on it if the behavior continued. Together with the AI team we determined that creating too much noice may reduce the confidence from the CISO, therefore increasing the possibilities of turning off the alerts altogether.

The name of the tab was also names originally "Alerts", but during user testing we discovered that some people assumed that all alert notifications would be located there, but the new view was only available for the CISO, so the final name for this version was "CISO".

Iteration of swipe to see option to snooze alert.

Final solution

Apart from the push notification, we also used the count on the badge to show the number of unread alerts, to let the user know that there is something unread ready for her to see any time.

Final design of badge count on app icon refering to unread alerts.

The final design is a list of alerts ordered by more recent on the top, each alert has some details, a signal to indicate if unread. The shortcut to mark as unread is to give quick access in the case that is clearly a false alarm, in the contrary case, and the alert is clearly real, the shortcut to share the info is useful.

Final design of list of alerts and swipe actions.

Given that the data was very consistent for this version, the search box could filter by name or location directly typing, the only filter needed was to show or hide archived alerts. The results had the same indication of unread if applied and the actions' menu to access any function available. This pattern on the search results is the same used on other views of the mobile UI.

Final design for search alerts.

The alert menu included the options to mark or unmark as unread to manage items similarly to email messages on the Mail tab, to archive alerts for false positives, to share the details using 3rd party apps or the built-in Mail function. This same menu is available from the Alert details page.

Final design of alert actions' menu.

The alert details' page showed all there is to know about the alert and offers all the available actions mentioned before. The idea is to pressent enough and clear information for the CISO to determine if the alert represent a valid risk and if that's the case to get in contact and share the details about the alert.

Final design of the alert full details page.

Reflection

Success metrics

The feature itself had a crucial success metric that affected UX, but it was out of my control, the relevancy of the alert, the fact that the information expressed the signal and removed the noice.

The design had the same success metrics as other projects, to be intuitive, useful and most importantly to ship on time.

Learnings

Personally, I learned about alerts from the perspective of UX and AI patterns, such as the signal to noice ratio or the push notification.

As a team, we were able to release the first of its kind and validate the idea of the offer to CISOs, this helped open many conversations with current customers about the types of alerts they specific organization migh be interested on.

Future iterations

Other alerts for different types of behaviors followed and are expected to continue to be added using the same framework to manage, consume and share the alert details.
